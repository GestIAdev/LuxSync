## THE KILL: WAVE 2122 POSTMORTEM

**Production log on ~126 BPM Boris Brejcha:**
```
GODEAR BPM trajectory: 85 → 84 → 84 → 162 → 169 → 169 → 169 → 169 → 169 → 96 → 92
```

**NEVER hit 126 BPM.** Pure octave hopping between sub-harmonics (84-96) and super-harmonics (162-173).

### ROOT CAUSE: Integer Lag Resolution

At production frame rate (46.4ms/frame = 2048 samples @ 44100 Hz):

| BPM | Beat Period (ms) | Lag (frames) | Math.round |
|-----|-----------------|-------------|-----------|
| 125 | 480.0 | 10.34 | **10** |
| 126 | 476.2 | 10.26 | **10** |
| 127 | 472.4 | 10.18 | **10** |
| 128 | 468.8 | 10.10 | **10** |
| 84  | 714.3 | 15.39 | **15** |
| 168 | 357.1 | 7.70  | **8** |

**Four BPMs collapse to the same integer lag (10).** Meanwhile, the sub-harmonic at lag 15 and super-harmonic at lag 8 have UNIQUE lags. In any autocorrelation where noise causes ±0.01 correlation jitter, the harmonic at a unique lag wins.

### WHY WAVE 2122 TESTS PASSED BUT PRODUCTION FAILED

Tests used `FRAME_DURATION_MS = 21ms` (overlap-based).
At 21ms/frame, 126 BPM = lag 22.7 frames → much better resolution.
Production uses 46.4ms/frame → lag 10.26 → **irrecoverable** with integer lags.

## THE FIX: TWO STANDARD DSP TECHNIQUES

### 1. Parabolic Interpolation (Sub-Sample Peak Resolution)

Instead of finding the best BPM by evaluating autocorrelation at pre-computed integer lags:

1. Compute R(lag) at ALL integer lags in [minLag, maxLag]
2. Find local maxima in R(lag) — these are the candidate periodicities
3. For each local max at lag k, fit a parabola through R(k-1), R(k), R(k+1)
4. Vertex formula: `δ = 0.5 * (R(k-1) - R(k+1)) / (R(k-1) - 2R(k) + R(k+1))`
5. Interpolated lag = k + δ → sub-frame precision
6. BPM = 60000 / (interpolatedLag × frameDurationMs)

**Result:** Integer lag 10 resolves to lag 10.26 → **exactly 126 BPM**.

This is the same technique used in:
- YIN pitch detection (Alain de Cheveigné)
- McLeod Pitch Method (MPM)
- Ableton's warping engine
- Every serious BPM analyzer

### 2. Harmonic Sieve (Octave Disambiguation)

Autocorrelation always produces peaks at multiples of the fundamental:
- If fundamental = lag 10 (126 BPM), peaks exist at lag 20, 30, 40...
- Sub-harmonics can also appear: lag 5 (252 BPM)

The harmonic sieve:
1. Collect ALL local maxima in the autocorrelation function
2. Sort by lag ascending (highest BPM first)
3. For each peak, check if a peak exists at ~2× its lag (harmonic support)
4. The fundamental = shortest lag that is:
   - Strong enough (≥75% of global max correlation)
   - Has harmonic support at ~2× lag

**Result:** Peak at lag 10 (126 BPM) has support at lag 20 (63 BPM) → confirmed as fundamental. Peak at lag 15 (84 BPM) has NO support at lag 30 → rejected. Peak at lag 8 (168 BPM) is checked but lag 10 wins because it's the shortest with support.

## LESSONS LEARNED

1. **ALWAYS TEST AT PRODUCTION FRAME RATE.** Tests at 21ms/frame have 2× the lag resolution of production at 46.4ms. A bug that's invisible at 21ms can be catastrophic at 46.4ms.

2. **Integer lags are insufficient for BPM detection at low frame rates.** The 120-130 BPM range collapses to a single integer lag at 46.4ms/frame. Parabolic interpolation is not optional — it's mandatory.

3. **Harmonic disambiguation via simple "check half/double" doesn't work.** The autocorrelation landscape has peaks at ALL harmonic multiples. You need a systematic sieve that evaluates the full peak structure.
